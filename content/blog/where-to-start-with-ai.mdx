## Begin with friction, not software

Ask where your team repeats the same low-judgement task, searches for the same information, or loses time moving work between people. Those patterns are more useful than a list of popular AI products.

## Choose a narrow first use case

A focused workflow is easier to test, safer to change, and clearer to measure. Examples might include turning meeting notes into structured actions or drafting a first response that a person reviews.

## Keep a person responsible

AI output needs ownership. Decide who checks accuracy, handles unusual situations, and can turn the system off. Useful automation supports judgement rather than hiding it.

## Measure the real change

Track time saved, error rates, adoption, and the quality of the handoff—not the novelty of the tool. Expand only when the evidence supports it.
